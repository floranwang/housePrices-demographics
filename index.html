However, even with the rise in prediction and data analysis tools, there have not been many projects that include demographics in their prediction calculation. In addition, there has tended to be more of a focus on predicting individual house values based on the houses features and amenities. Research and visualizations that have included demographic data with housing have tended to focus on one particular city or region. Our project hopes to get a broader picture of house values and demographics in order to possibly notice trends across counties or states.

House values (as measured by the Zillow Home Value Index) and demographics have changed significantly between 2008 and 2016. The decreases in the overall percent change in house value between 2008 and 2012 occurred during the time of the recession and the housing bubble bursting. The increases in average percent change in house value between 2012 and 2016 coincide with the subsequent economic boom.

This trend is not necessarily true uniformly across the country. Different counties in different parts of the country show that there is a mix of increases and decreases in house value. This may be because different states and counties have localized changes that may affect percent change in house value or a particular demographic group. For instance, different metropolitan areas have experienced gentrification, particularly in the recent years. Along with gentrification comes an influx of wealth, increase house value prices, and displacement of people who cannot afford to live in the area. However, there is a general trend of a increasing population for most counties in the United States.

An Index Analysis

How do we compare the relationship between change in house value and demographics?

One way of doing this is through an index.


Index-population = % Change in House Value ⁄ % Change in Total Population

Index-race = % Change in House Value ⁄ % Change in Race


Each index value is a ratio between the percent change in house value and the percent change in the demographic group. A ratio of between 0 and 1 means that the percent change in house value is significantly lower than the percent change in the demographic group. A negative index value means that the percent change in house value and the percent change in the demographic group is not in the same direction- one value is positive, and the other is negative. A positive index value means that the percent change in house value and the percent change in the demographic group is either both positive or both negative.

For the total population index, most of the country shows a fairly low index score, meaning that there is not a strong correlation between percent change in house value and percent change in total population. However, Illinois and Rhode Island have strong negative index values, indicating a negative correlation between percent change in total population and percent change in house value. In the case of Illinois, with an index value of -31.3, there has been a 0.1% decrease in average home value but a slight increase in population. Similarly Rhode Island, with an index value of -360.0, experienced a 0.06% decrease in house value but a very small increase in population.

In the map of indices for the Black population, there were lower index values for most states. California had the most extreme index value of 14.67. In this case, there was a 0.8% average increase in home value and a slight increase in the percent change in the Black population.

For the index of the Asian population, essentially all the states have low index values (as shown by the virtually gray map). Upon closer inspection of individual states in the comparison tool, there are many states that high percent increases in their Asian population. For example, although there was a 0.08% increase in house value in California, there was also a 0.1976% increase in the Asian population- a value more than double than the percent increase in house value.

For the index of the White population, there are a handful of states on the east coast with more extreme index values. Massachusetts and Maryland both have very high positive index values. Interestingly, there is an opposite average percent change in house value- MA at 0.1079% and MD at -0.1465%. In addition, there is virtually zero average percent change in the White population in both states. This is what causes the index value to be so great. The index distribution for other racial groups highlights the outliers in Colorado and Nebraska. Again, we see a a moderate to high percent change in house value and a minuscule percent change in the Other demographic group.
